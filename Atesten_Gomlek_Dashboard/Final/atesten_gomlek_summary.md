In Halide Edib Adıvar’s seminal national epic, the geographical journey from the decadent, occupied salons of Istanbul to the dust-choked, blood-drenched steppes of Anatolia serves as a physical manifestation of a nation’s spiritual rebirth. At the heart of this narrative is Peyami, a paralyzed, alienated intellectual recovering in Ankara’s Cebeci Hospital with a bullet lodged in his skull. Through his feverish memoirs, we witness the agonizing birth of modern Turkey, characterized by a love triangle that is inextricably bound to the defense of the homeland—a psychological state the characters term the *ateşten gömlek* (the shirt of flame).

The narrative begins in Istanbul, a city paralyzed by the humiliation of Allied occupation. Standing on the Beşiktaş coast, Peyami watches foreign warships breach the Bosphorus, feeling a bitter envy for his military compatriots: the stoic, elegant İhsan and the rugged Major Cemal. For these men, the cosmopolitan, Europeanized salons of Şişli—epitomized by Peyami’s anti-nationalist mother and the arrogant British representative, Mister Cook—represent a suffocating moral decay. The catalyst for their awakening arrives in the form of Ayşe, Cemal’s cousin, who flees to Istanbul after her husband and child are slaughtered during the Greek invasion of Izmir. Veiled in black and bearing a wounded arm, Ayşe becomes the emotional and symbolic heart of the resistance. She is the physical embodiment of the occupied motherland; to love her is to join the national struggle. At the historic Sultanahmet rally, her silent grief galvanizes the masses, transforming Peyami’s intellectual alienation into a desperate, patriotic fervor.

As the British consolidate their grip on Istanbul, the narrative undergoes a vital geographical shift. Escaping across the water to Anatolia, the protagonists leave behind the safety of metropolitan cafes for the rugged wilderness of Kandıra and Geyve. This physical exodus is a baptism of fire. Disguised as peasants, driving oxcarts through bandit-infested reeds, Peyami, İhsan, and Ayşe shed their old identities. In Geyve and Sarılar, they confront the chaotic reality of the National Struggle: smuggling weapons from hidden caches, navigating the treachery of local militias, and training raw recruits. Anatolia is depicted not as a pastoral idyll, but as a harsh, demanding crucible. Here, the wild village girl Kezban—whose unrequited, fierce love for İhsan mirrors the untamed spirit of the Anatolian soil—contrasts with Ayşe’s refined but resolute patriotism.

The climax of this journey unfolds on the central frontlines of Eskişehir and Polatlı. Amidst the horrific carnage of the military hospitals, Ayşe acts as a ministering angel, tending to the dying while remaining a distant, unattainable ideal. Both İhsan and Peyami are consumed by their love for her—a passion that tortures them as deeply as the war itself. Under the thunder of Greek artillery on the yellow ridges of Karadağ, the tragedy reaches its zenith. Ayşe is killed by a stray shell while treating the wounded, and a devastated İhsan is mortally injured leading his men in a desperate bayonet charge. Peyami catches his dying friend in his arms, and the two lovers are buried side by side in the dusty cemetery of Basri Village.

In the shattering epilogue back at Cebeci Hospital, Peyami dies on the operating table during surgery to extract the bullet from his brain. The novel concludes with a devastating post-mortem twist: the attending doctors investigate his files and find no record of a nurse named Ayşe or an officer named İhsan. Ultimately, whether this epic of love, jealousy, and sacrifice was a historical reality or the feverish, redemptive hallucination of a dying soldier is irrelevant. The "shirt of flame" remains the defining metaphor for a generation that willingly consumed itself so that a new nation might rise from the ashes.
